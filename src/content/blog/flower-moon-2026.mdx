# Flower Moon 2026: When the May Full Moon Peaks in the UK and Why May Has Two

**Quick answer:** The 2026 Flower Moon peaks on Friday, 1 May at 17:23 UTC, which is 18:23 BST in the UK. May 2026 has two full moons - the Flower Moon on 1 May and a Blue Moon on 31 May. The name comes from spring flowers blooming across the Northern Hemisphere. The moon is not guaranteed to look pink or floral - any warm colours near the horizon are caused by atmosphere, not the moon itself.

Every May, the full moon picks up the name Flower Moon, and every year thousands of people search for when it happens and how to see it. In 2026, May is unusual because it has two full moons, making the second one a Blue Moon. Here is everything you need to know about the timing, the meaning and what to look for in the sky.

## When is the Flower Moon in 2026?

The Flower Moon falls on **Friday, 1 May 2026**. It reaches peak fullness at **17:23 UTC**, which is **18:23 BST** in the UK.

You do not need to catch the exact minute. The Moon looks full to the naked eye for roughly a day either side of the precise peak. So the evenings of both 30 April and 1 May are good times to look.

If you miss 1 May entirely, May 2026 gives you a second chance. Another full moon follows on 31 May.

May 2026 has two full moons. Here are the details:

|---|---|---|---|---|

Both dates are worth marking if you enjoy skywatching. The Flower Moon on 1 May arrives in the early evening, making it easier to spot around moonrise in the UK. The Blue Moon on 31 May peaks in the morning, so the best UK viewing would be the night before or after.

## Does May 2026 have two full moons?

Yes. The lunar cycle takes about 29.5 days, so when a full moon falls right at the start of a month, there is just enough time for another before the month ends.

In May 2026, the first full moon lands on 1 May and the second on 31 May. The second full moon in a single calendar month is commonly called a **Blue Moon**. Despite the name, it does not look blue. It looks exactly like any other full moon.

Two full moons in one month are not especially rare, but they do not happen every year. It is worth knowing about because the phrase "once in a blue moon" comes from this idea.

## What is the Flower Moon?

The Flower Moon is the traditional name for the full moon that falls in May. The name refers to the widespread blooming of flowers across the Northern Hemisphere during spring.

Almanac sources such as The Old Farmer's Almanac commonly list Flower Moon alongside other monthly full moon names - Wolf Moon in January, Snow Moon in February, Worm Moon in March, and so on. These names have roots in various cultural and farming traditions.

The Flower Moon is not a special astronomical event. It is simply a full moon that happens to fall in May. What makes it appealing is the timing: warm spring evenings, longer daylight, and a bright moon rising against a colourful sky.

## Why is it called the Flower Moon?

The name reflects what is happening on the ground, not in the sky. By early May in the Northern Hemisphere, wildflowers, garden plants and trees are in full bloom. The full moon during this period picked up the name Flower Moon because it coincides with that burst of spring colour.

There is no single official origin for the name. It appears in several almanac and cultural traditions, all linked to the same seasonal idea.

## What is special about the May 2026 Flower Moon?

Several things make the May 2026 Flower Moon worth noting:

- It is the **first of two full moons** in May 2026.
- It falls on **1 May**, early enough for a second full moon before the month ends.
- Some astronomy sources describe it as a **micromoon** because the Moon is near apogee.
- It peaks at **18:23 BST**, during the evening in the UK, which makes moonrise viewing convenient.
- The second May full moon on 31 May is a **Blue Moon**.

None of this makes it a once-in-a-lifetime event, but it is a good month for moon watching.

## What colour is the Flower Moon?

The Flower Moon is **not guaranteed to look pink, floral or any special colour**. Despite the name, "Flower Moon" refers to the season, not the moon's appearance.

That said, the Moon often looks golden, orange or amber when it is low on the horizon. This happens because moonlight passes through more of Earth's atmosphere near the horizon, scattering blue light and letting warmer tones through. It is the same reason sunsets look orange.

If you want to see the warmest colours, watch the Moon as it rises. The higher it climbs, the whiter it tends to appear.

## What is the phase of the moon in May 2026?

May 2026 includes two full moons:

- **1 May** - Full Moon (Flower Moon)
- **31 May** - Full Moon (Blue Moon)

Between these dates, the Moon passes through its standard cycle of waning gibbous, last quarter, waning crescent, new moon, waxing crescent, first quarter and waxing gibbous before reaching full again.

## Is the May 2026 Flower Moon a micromoon?

Some astronomy sources, including Star Walk, describe the 1 May 2026 full moon as a **micromoon**. This means it occurs when the Moon is near its farthest point from Earth in its orbit, called **apogee**.

A micromoon can appear about 4 to 5 per cent smaller and slightly dimmer than an average full moon. In practice, the difference is hard to spot without comparing photographs. The Moon will still look full, bright and impressive to the naked eye.

A micromoon is the opposite of a supermoon, which happens when the full moon coincides with the Moon being near its closest point to Earth (perigee).

## How to see the Flower Moon in the UK

Here are practical tips for UK viewers on 1 May 2026:

- **Check your local moonrise time** - it varies across the UK. Use a site such as Timeanddate to find the exact time for your town.
- **Face east around moonrise** - the Moon rises in the east and climbs higher through the evening.
- **Choose a clear view of the horizon** - avoid buildings, trees and hills that block the low sky.
- **Move away from bright streetlights** - less light pollution means a clearer view, though the Moon is bright enough to see from almost anywhere.
- **Use binoculars for detail** - you can see craters and surface features even with basic binoculars.
- **Check the weather forecast** - cloud cover is the biggest obstacle. If 1 May is cloudy, try 30 April or 2 May instead.
- **Photograph the Moon when it is low** - this is when it often looks largest and warmest in colour.

## Key background

A **full moon** happens when the Moon is on the opposite side of Earth from the Sun, so sunlight fully illuminates the face we see. It occurs roughly every 29.5 days.

**Traditional full moon names** come from various almanac and cultural sources. Each month has its own name tied to seasonal events - wolves howling in January, flowers blooming in May, harvests in September.

A **Blue Moon** is the second full moon in a calendar month. It has nothing to do with colour. The phrase "once in a blue moon" means something uncommon, because most months have only one full moon.

A **micromoon** is a full moon that occurs near apogee. It looks slightly smaller and dimmer than average. A **supermoon** is the opposite - a full moon near perigee, which looks slightly larger and brighter.

**Moonrise** is often the best time to watch a full moon. When the Moon is near the horizon, it can look larger (an optical illusion called the Moon illusion) and warmer in colour. As it climbs higher, it appears smaller and brighter white.
